Computer Vision on the Jobsite: What We Learned After 90 Days

PPE detection, unsafe behavior alerts, and quality defect catches — real numbers from a real site. Here's what worked, what surprised us, and what we'd do differently.

When we deployed computer vision cameras across a Charlotte trail infrastructure project last fall, we had high expectations — but also real skepticism from the crew. "It feels like surveillance," one foreman told us. By week 4, he was the system's biggest advocate.

The Numbers After 90 Days

  • 847 PPE compliance checks logged — 94% compliance rate by week 8 (up from 71% at start)
  • 23 quality defect flags caught in-phase vs. post-installation — estimated $34,000 in rework savings
  • 6 unsafe behavior alerts near heavy equipment — zero incidents in the 90-day period
  • 2 subcontractor performance disputes resolved using timestamped video evidence

What Surprised Us

The biggest surprise wasn't the safety data — it was how the system changed behavior proactively. Workers who knew cameras were active started self-correcting before supervisors ever saw an alert. The compliance rate improvement from 71% to 94% happened mostly in the first three weeks, as the crew internalized that the system was watching consistently.

The second surprise was the subcontractor accountability angle. When a dispute arose about whether a specific installation had been completed before weather damage, we pulled the timestamped footage and resolved it in 20 minutes. That alone saved an estimated $12,000 in potential litigation costs.

We break down the real numbers — upfront premiums, long-term savings, and the Charlotte incentives most developers don't know about.

The most common objection we hear when sustainable building comes up: "It costs too much." And sometimes, when you look at day-one construction costs in isolation, that's technically true. But that framing misses the full picture entirely.

The Real Premium

LEED-aligned construction in Charlotte typically adds 2–8% to upfront costs depending on project type. For a $2M commercial build, that's $40,000–$160,000 more at signing. That number sounds significant until you run the 10-year math.

How AI Trail Kiosks Are Changing the Way Charlotte Manages Public Parks

From usage analytics to emergency alerts — the data parks departments are now collecting that they never had before, and what they're doing with it.

Before smart kiosks, Charlotte's parks department managed trail systems essentially blind. They knew how many parking passes were sold. They had occasional manual trail counters. And they had the complaint calls — always reactive, never predictive.

After deploying TrueServe's AI kiosk network on a 12-mile trail system, the data picture changed completely within 60 days.

What the Data Revealed

  • Peak usage was 6–8am on weekdays, not weekends — changing when maintenance crews were scheduled
  • One trail connector had 40% of all user traffic but zero signage — a quick fix with major impact
  • 3 restroom facilities were consistently flagged in user feedback as needing maintenance — addressed proactively
  • Emergency button was pressed twice in 90 days — both times resulting in faster response than phone 911 would have allowed

The Broader Smart City Play

What makes TrueServe's kiosk platform more than just a wayfinding tool is that it generates continuous, structured data that feeds directly into Charlotte's smart city infrastructure planning. Every interaction, every route choice, every feedback submission builds a picture of how people actually use public space — not how planners assumed they would.

That data becomes the evidence base for the next round of infrastructure investment. Which trails need expansion. Where new connections add the most value. Which parks are underutilized and why.
